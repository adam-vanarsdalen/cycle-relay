## 1. Cycle Clarity

### What It Does at Each Step

**3D Ultrasound + AI Follicle Measurement (Follicle Clarity)**
- Performs 3D transvaginal ultrasound sweeps of both ovaries
- AI detects and measures every follicle in 10 seconds per ovary (< 1 minute total scan)
- Manual measurement takes 10–20 minutes; Cycle Clarity cuts this to ~3 minutes total patient time
- Measurement variation: 1.4% between ultrasonographers (vs. high human inconsistency)
- 19,776 ovaries annotated, 91,782 follicles validated across 5 clinical trials before FDA clearance

**MyCycleClarity Portal**
- Software platform that receives follicle data and displays complete folliculograms to clinical team
- Physicians review ultrasounds anytime, 24/7
- Automated follicular report emailed to the patient after every scan (Coastal Fertility uses this)
- Shows follicle counts, individual sizes, bilateral summaries

**EMR Transmission**
- Follicular data sent downstream to EHR/EMR for documentation
- Integrates with clinic scheduling and staffing for workload forecasting

**Trigger Timing Predictions**
- Uses follicle size data + estradiol trends to predict optimal trigger timing
- Helps physician decide when follicles are mature enough for retrieval (target: ≥18mm leading follicles)

### What Data It Produces
- Follicle counts per ovary (left/right)
- Individual follicle sizes in millimeters
- Folliculogram (complete visual map of both ovaries)
- Estradiol (E2) trend support (lab values entered separately by clinic)
- Trigger timing predictions
- Downstream analytics for lab workload management

### What It Does NOT Do
- **Does not generate personalized patient communications** after physician review
- Does not explain the physician's dosing decision to the patient
- Does not handle the emotional conversation that follows each monitoring visit
- Does not schedule next appointments or call patients with instructions
- Does not manage the coordinator-patient relationship post-scan

### The Workflow Gap Cycle Relay Closes

```
Cycle Clarity automated follicular report → emailed to patient (what follicles measured)
        ↓
Physician reviews data → makes dosing adjustment decision (private, clinical)
        ↓
[GAP] → Coordinator manually communicates decision to each patient via phone/portal
        ↓
Patient receives call: medication change, next appointment, what to expect, reassurance
```

Cycle Clarity automates the measurement. The communication of the *meaning* of those measurements to the patient—tailored, compassionate, stage-appropriate—remains entirely manual. At a busy clinic seeing dozens of monitoring patients per day, this is where coordinators spend hours.

## 3. IVF Coordinator Workflow

### Daily Structure
**Morning (7am–12pm)**
- Morning monitoring coordination: patient check-ins, scan scheduling, queue management
- Processing overnight/early lab results (E2, progesterone)
- Uploading monitoring results to EHR
- Reviewing Cycle Clarity folliculograms with physician

**Afternoon (12pm–5pm)**
- Result calls: calling each monitoring patient with dosing instructions, next appointment
- Medication adjustments: relaying physician-ordered medication changes
- Pharmacy coordination: prescription calls, prior auth, medication availability
- Appointment scheduling for next monitoring, retrieval, transfer

**Throughout Day**
- Inbound patient questions: anxiety, medication confusion, side effects
- Urgent: trigger night instructions (time-critical, must be exact)
- Documentation in EHR

### The 14 IVF Cycle Stages

#### Stage 1: Day 3 Baseline
- **What happens**: Patient comes in on cycle day 3 for baseline ultrasound (antral follicle count) and bloodwork (FSH, E2, AMH)
- **Coordinator communication**: Confirm medication protocol start, review injection training, set expectations for stimulation phase
- **Emotional context**: Mix of hope and anxiety; this may be patient's first or Nth cycle; nervous about starting
- **What patient needs**: Clear instructions for medication start date/time, reassurance, what to expect next

#### Stage 2: Stimulation Day 3–5 (Early Response)
- **What happens**: First monitoring after stimulation starts — checking ovaries are responding, E2 rising appropriately
- **Coordinator communication**: Confirm medication is working, relay any dose adjustments, schedule next monitoring
- **Emotional context**: Cautious optimism; patients anxiously counting follicles
- **What patient needs**: Reassurance that early response is normal, what the numbers mean in accessible terms

#### Stage 3: Stimulation Day 6–8 (Mid Response)
- **What happens**: Follicles growing, closer monitoring, E2 typically 300–1500+ depending on response
- **Coordinator communication**: Update on growth, any medication adjustments, next appointment, watch for OHSS signs
- **Emotional context**: Anxiety intensifying; patients comparing to last cycle or online forums
- **What patient needs**: Validation that growth is on track, clear numbers without clinical overload

#### Stage 4: Stimulation Day 9+ (Approaching Trigger)
- **What happens**: Leading follicles approaching 17–18mm; physician evaluating trigger timing; more frequent monitoring
- **Coordinator communication**: "Getting close" messaging, possible trigger tonight instruction, preparing patient for retrieval
- **Emotional context**: High excitement/anxiety; retrieval feels real; fear of premature ovulation
- **What patient needs**: Clear timeline, what trigger means, what to expect retrieval day

#### Stage 5: Trigger Night (Time-Critical HCG/Lupron Instructions)
- **What happens**: Physician confirms trigger shot time; patient must inject at exact time (34–36hr pre-retrieval window)
- **Coordinator communication**: EXACT time to inject, which medication, retrieval time/date, NPO instructions, arrival time, no-sex instructions
- **Emotional context**: High stakes, some panic; patients terrified of making a mistake
- **What patient needs**: Extremely clear, unambiguous instructions; time repeated; what happens if confused; emergency contact
- **Tone**: Urgent, precise, warm but not casual — this is the most time-sensitive communication in the cycle

#### Stage 6: Retrieval Day Pre-Procedure
- **What happens**: Patient arrives for egg retrieval; NPO confirmed, IV sedation prep
- **Coordinator communication**: Arrival instructions, what to expect (sedation, procedure, recovery), partner/sperm sample instructions
- **Emotional context**: High anxiety, some excitement; fear of anesthesia, pain, low egg count
- **What patient needs**: Step-by-step what to expect, reassurance, practical logistics

#### Stage 7: Retrieval Day Post-Procedure
- **What happens**: Eggs retrieved, count confirmed in OR, patient in recovery
- **Coordinator communication**: Egg count, next steps (fertilization), medication changes (progesterone/estrogen support), when to expect fertilization report
- **Emotional context**: Relief mixed with new anxiety about fertilization; egg count can be disappointing or reassuring
- **What patient needs**: Egg count framed compassionately, realistic fertilization expectations, clear medication instructions, timeline

#### Stage 8: Fertilization Report (Day 1 Post-Retrieval)
- **What happens**: Lab checks for fertilization ~17 hours post-retrieval; 2PN embryos = normally fertilized
- **Coordinator communication**: Number fertilized of eggs retrieved, what happens next (Day 3 or Day 5 culture)
- **Emotional context**: "The first cut" — any loss of eggs to fertilization is emotional; patients often do math anxiously
- **What patient needs**: Clear number with compassionate framing; not all eggs fertilize normally (this is normal); next milestone

#### Stage 9: Day 3 Embryo Update
- **What happens**: Embryos checked for cell division (typically 6–8 cells); some may have arrested
- **Coordinator communication**: Number still developing, cell count, any grading info, heading to blast culture
- **Emotional context**: Attrition is normal but feels like loss; patients Google "6-cell embryo success rates" at 2am
- **What patient needs**: Reassurance that attrition is expected and normal; focus on blastocyst milestone ahead

#### Stage 10: Blastocyst Report (Day 5/6)
- **What happens**: Embryos that reach blastocyst stage are counted; PGT-A testing if planned
- **Coordinator communication**: Number of blasts, quality grading, freeze confirmation, PGT-A sample sent, timeline to transfer
- **Emotional context**: High stakes — blast count = potential babies; low blast count is devastating
- **What patient needs**: Numbers with compassionate context; explanation of grading; next steps clearly stated

#### Stage 11: Transfer Day
- **What happens**: Embryo(s) transferred to uterus; typically 1–2 embryos; PGT-A normal embryo selected
- **Coordinator communication**: Transfer confirmation, post-transfer restrictions (activity, medications), TWW expectations, beta date
- **Emotional context**: Cautious joy, superstition; patients want to do everything "right" to help implantation
- **What patient needs**: Clear post-transfer instructions; what to avoid; reassurance that normal activity is fine; beta date clearly stated

#### Stage 12: TWW Day 7 Check-in (Two-Week Wait)
- **What happens**: Mid-wait progesterone check; no result yet, just support
- **Coordinator communication**: Progesterone level confirmation, continued medication instructions, countdown to beta
- **Emotional context**: Peak anxiety; patients symptom-spotting obsessively; days feel like weeks
- **What patient needs**: Compassionate check-in; normalize the anxiety; affirm that symptoms (or lack of) are not predictive; beta date reminder

#### Stage 13: Beta Result — Positive
- **What happens**: Beta hCG positive; pregnancy confirmed
- **Coordinator communication**: Positive result, level context, continued medications, first OB ultrasound scheduling
- **Emotional context**: Joy mixed with fear (many IVF patients have experienced loss); cautious celebration
- **What patient needs**: Warm congratulations, clear next steps (meds, OB scan scheduling), normalized caution

#### Stage 14: Beta Result — Negative
- **What happens**: Beta hCG negative; cycle failed
- **Coordinator communication**: Result, compassionate acknowledgment, what happens next (physician consult for next steps)
- **Emotional context**: Grief, devastation; one of the hardest moments in a person's reproductive journey
- **What patient needs**: Extreme compassion; no clinical pivot immediately; space for grief; clear but gentle next steps; physician follow-up scheduled
- **Tone**: Grief-aware, human, warm — this message will be read and re-read in a very dark moment

## 4. HIPAA Communication Requirements

- Patient portal is primary compliant channel (encrypted, authenticated)
- Email can be used with patient consent but should not contain detailed PHI without encryption
- Phone calls are HIPAA-compliant if identity verified
- AI-generated drafts must be reviewed by coordinator before sending — no autonomous AI-to-patient delivery
- All patient communications must be documented in EHR
- AI tool must not store PHI — generation is stateless, coordinator reviews and sends manually

## 5. Competitive Landscape

### What Exists
- **Conversational AI chatbots** (FAQ handling, appointment reminders): Meditab, Pabau, PatientPrism
- **IVF EHR platforms** with messaging: eIVF, Softclinic, Vitrisoft — basic templated messages
- **Fertility Bridge** conversational AI: front-of-funnel engagement (pre-treatment), not cycle-specific
- **General medical AI scribes**: note-taking during consults, not coordinator communication
- **Automated SMS/WhatsApp reminders**: scheduling, not clinical communication

### The Gap
None of the above tools generate:
- Stage-specific, emotionally-calibrated patient messages
- Messages that incorporate actual monitoring data (follicle counts, E2, medication adjustments)
- Messages that match the emotional register of each cycle stage
- Messages that output a coordinator summary and clinical flags for review
- A HIPAA-safe local deployment option (Ollama)

The closest analogy is an AI scribe that listens and generates notes — but for the *outbound patient communication* side of the coordinator's workflow, no purpose-built tool exists at this granularity.

### Positioning Statement for Cycle Relay
Cycle Relay is the missing downstream layer: where Cycle Clarity ends (AI-standardized follicle data delivered to the physician), Cycle Relay begins — translating the physician's decision into a compassionate, stage-aware patient message the coordinator can review and send in seconds.
